Supported formats

If your iPhone can open it,
Raza Photos can too.

Raza Photos uses Apple's own frameworks to read and display images and videos. The supported format list is Apple's, not ours — which means it stays current as Apple adds new formats.

Photos

Any file that Apple's system recognises as an image is accepted. In practice, that means every format your iPhone camera produces and every format Photos.app can open.

HEIC / HEIF Apple's default photo format since iPhone 7. Efficient, high quality, full HDR support.
JPEG The universal standard. Every camera, every phone, every decade.
PNG Lossless, with transparency. Screenshots and graphics.
TIFF Professional archival format, full metadata support.
PSD Photoshop documents. Metadata is read and written.
AVIF Next-generation format. Read support — metadata changes are stored in the library.
WebP Google's modern format, supported on macOS Ventura and iOS 14 and later.
GIF Static and animated. Shown as a still frame.
JPEG 2000 Legacy high-quality format used in some professional workflows.
BMP Windows bitmap format, read as a regular image.
What about RAW?

RAW formats from cameras (DNG, CR2, ARW, NEF, and others) are not supported. Raza Photos is built around your photo library — the kind of files your iPhone and Photos.app produce — not a professional RAW workflow.

Videos

Raza Photos plays the videos that come from your camera roll. It uses Apple's AVFoundation framework directly, so any format your iPhone records or your Apple device natively supports will play correctly.

MOV Apple's QuickTime container. Used by iPhones and Macs for recorded video.
MP4 The universal video standard. Works everywhere, plays natively.
M4V Apple's iTunes video container.
HEVC / H.265 Apple's efficient codec for 4K and HDR video. Used on all modern iPhones.
H.264 The workhorse codec. Compatible with everything.
ProRes Apple's professional lossless codec. Supported on iPhone 13 Pro and later.
This is not a movie player

Raza Photos is a photo library app that also handles your camera roll videos. It will not open MKV, AVI, WMV, or other formats associated with downloaded movies or video editing. If a format is not natively supported by Apple's frameworks, it will not play.

HDR — photos and video

Raza Photos renders HDR content using Apple's native display pipeline. On supported displays — iPhone 12 and later, MacBook Pro with Liquid Retina XDR, Pro Display XDR — photos and videos appear with the full brightness and colour range they were captured in.

How gain maps work

Most HDR photos are not actually stored in HDR. They contain a standard SDR image — which looks correct on every screen — plus a hidden gain map attached to the same file. The gain map is a set of instructions that tells a capable display how to boost specific areas to full HDR brightness.

On a standard display, the SDR image is shown and the gain map is ignored. On an HDR-capable display, the gain map is applied on top of the SDR image, and the photo lights up the way it looked when you took it.

Raza Photos passes the complete file — gain map included — directly to Apple's display framework. Nothing is decoded or composited by us. The display does the work.

Apple Adaptive HDR HEIC files with an embedded Apple gain map. Every photo taken on iPhone 12 and later.
Ultra HDR JPEG JPEG files with an ISO 21496-1 gain map. Produced by iPhone cameras since iOS 17.
Dolby Vision Dynamic HDR metadata in MOV and MP4 video. Shot on iPhone 12 Pro and later.
HDR10 Static HDR metadata in video files.
HLG Hybrid Log-Gamma, used in broadcast HDR video.

Live Photos

Live Photos are not really a file format. They are two files — a still image and a short video clip — that belong together. Here is how that works in practice.

What a Live Photo actually is

When you take a Live Photo, your iPhone captures 1.5 seconds of video before and after the shutter moment, alongside the still image. Inside Apple Photos, you see one item. On disk, it is always two separate files: a HEIC for the still and a MOV for the motion. They are linked by a shared identifier hidden in the metadata of both files.

To get Live Photos out of Apple Photos with the link intact, you need to export using File → Export Unmodified Originals. This preserves the metadata identifier that connects the two files. A regular export will produce just the still image.

📷

You export from Apple Photos

Using File → Export Unmodified Originals. For each Live Photo, you get two files in the same folder: IMG_1234.HEIC and IMG_1234.MOV.

🔍

Raza Photos reads both files

When it imports the folder, it reads the shared Live Photo identifier from the HEIC's Apple MakerNote metadata and from the MOV's QuickTime metadata. Files with a matching identifier are recognised as a pair.

🔗

The pair is linked automatically

The MOV disappears from your grid — it is not shown as a separate video. Instead, the HEIC entry becomes a Live Photo. The motion plays when you tap or hover, exactly as it does in Apple Photos.

✅

Nothing to configure

No special folder structure, no naming convention. As long as both files are in the same imported folder and the metadata identifier is intact, the pairing is automatic.

If the live photo plays as a still

This usually means the MOV companion file is missing. It can happen if you copied only the HEIC from Apple Photos, or exported using a format other than Unmodified Original. Re-export using File → Export Unmodified Originals and import again.
